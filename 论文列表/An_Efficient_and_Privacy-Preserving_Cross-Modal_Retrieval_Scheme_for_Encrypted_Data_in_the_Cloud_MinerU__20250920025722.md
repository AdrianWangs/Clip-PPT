# An Efficient and Privacy-Preserving Cross-Modal Retrieval Scheme for Encrypted Data in the Cloud

Wei Jiang1,2,3, Yang Li1,2,3\*, Bo Li1,2, Zixin Tang1,2,3, Jinchao Zhang1,2, and Chuanrong Li1,2 1Institute of Information Engineering, Chinese Academy of Sciences 2Key Laboratory of Cybersecurity Security Defense 3School of Cyber Security, University of Chinese Academy of Sciences Beijing, China Email: {jiangwei, liyang, libo, tangzixin, zhangjinchao, lichuanrong}@iie.ac.cn

Abstract- The rapid development and widespread adoption of cloud computing have made privacy- preserving data retrieval in the cloud a hot topic in privacy computing research. While numerous schemes have been proposed for privacy- preserving text- to- text and image- to- image retrieval, there is a lack of research on cross- modal retrieval with privacy protection in the cloud, leaving ample room for enhancing the security and efficiency of existing schemes. To address these challenges, this paper proposes a scheme for privacy- preserving cross- modal retrieval. The proposed scheme leverages a cross- modal pre- trained model to extract features from both text and images, which are then mapped into hash buckets using locality- sensitive hashing (LSH), thereby enhancing retrieval efficiency. Additionally, a data structure is employed to generate secure indexes for these hash buckets via symmetric searchable encryption (SSE). To further improve retrieval accuracy, a secure k- nearest neighbor (kNN) algorithm is applied for second- round ranking of the retrieval results. Analysis and extensive experiments on widely- used real- world datasets have demonstrated the scheme's effectiveness in preserving privacy while maintaining efficiency and feasibility.

Keywords- privacy- preserving, locality- sensitive hashing, searchable encryption, cross- modal retrieval

# I. INTRODUCTION

With the rapid advancement of the Internet and the widespread adoption of smart devices such as smartphones and tablets, multimedia data- particularly in the form of images and text- has experienced explosive growth. Many enterprises and individuals have chosen to outsource their data to cloud platforms, leveraging these platforms' powerful storage and computational capabilities to reduce the costs associated with data storage and processing. However, this model of storing and processing data in the cloud exposes users' private information to potential privacy breaches. As a result, ensuring accurate and efficient cross- modal data retrieval while preserving data privacy has become an urgent research challenge.

Existing plaintext cross- modal retrieval systems have achieved impressive results, with OpenAI's Contrastive Language- Image Pre- training (CLIP) [1] model being one of the most notable examples. CLIP's joint embedding spaces and contrastive learning provide it with strong zero- shot capabilities, making it one of the state- of- the- art models in this domain. The CLIP model maps both images and texts into a shared vector space, and after normalization, the inner product of the text and image features represents the similarity between the text and the image. Currently, many plaintext cross- modal retrieval solutions based on CLIP exist [2,3]. However, directly applying plaintext cross- modal retrieval schemes in cloud environments inevitably leads to privacy leakage. Current schemes for searchable encryption and secure content- based image retrieval primarily address text- to- text or image- to- image retrieval, but not cross- modal retrieval. Song et al.[4] first introduced symmetric searchable encryption (SSE), but this scheme requires scanning the entire document, resulting in significant overhead. Curtmola et al. [5] introduced the concept of adaptive security and proposed an inverted index- based method to improve query performance. Golle et al. [6] first proposed a symmetric searchable encryption scheme supporting multi- keyword Boolean searches. Li et al. [7] used convolutional neural network (CNN) for image feature extraction in their secure content- based image retrieval scheme, and developed hierarchical index trees to facilitate efficient searches. Mao et al.'s [8] scheme supports multi- owner, multi- user settings with lightweight access control, employing Chameleon Hashing for dynamic result verification. Yang et al. [9] introduced MUTEIR, which utilizes the Distributed Two Trapdoors Public- Key Cryptosystem (DT- PKC) technique to implement a multi- key mechanism and embeds watermarks into encrypted images to enable traceability by malicious users.

There are relatively few existing privacy- preserving cross- modal retrieval schemes and the current solutions have certain limitations. Guo et al. [10] proposed SCMR, which combines locality- sensitive hashing (LSH) with the Paillier homomorphic encryption algorithm to address the issue of privacy- preserving cross- modal retrieval. However, both indexing and querying in SCMR use Paillier encryption, and the server performs matching under homomorphic encryption, which leads to low accuracy in query results due to the randomness introduced by the Paillier algorithm. Wang et al. [11] introduced a two- branch feature extraction network based on CNN for the cloud server to extract features from encrypted cross- modal data. However, in Wang et al.'s PPCMR, a transposition cipher is used to encrypt documents and images, but the number of encryption keys is equal to the number of user files, which imposes a significant key management burden on users. Additionally, the server needs to extract features from the encrypted documents and images, but it is difficult to align their semantics when encrypted under neural networks. PITR [12], proposed by Zhang et al., fine- tunes the pre- trained model and uses the Hierarchical Navigable Small World Graphs (HNSW) algorithm during secure cross- modal retrieval for approximate nearest neighbor (ANN) search. In

PITR, the index generated by the HNSW algorithm is a multilayer graph structure, and the user needs to generate this index locally, which imposes a large computational load, making it unsuitable for big data scenarios.

In response to the limitations of existing solutions, this paper proposes a privacy- preserving cross- modal retrieval scheme. The scheme first utilizes CLIP to extract features from images and texts, mapping them into the same vector space to bridge the "semantic gap" between cross- modal data. To achieve fast retrieval in large- scale data scenarios, our scheme applies LSH to obtain the hash values of features, enabling the rapid filtering of irrelevant data. Based on this, the scheme introduces SSE and secure  $\mathrm{k}$  - nearest neighbor (kNN), creating a two- layer secure index. Specifically, the user's feature hash values are split and reorganized into multiple independent inverted indexes. Each inverted index is then encrypted using inverted index- based SSE, forming the first layer of the index. To further improve search accuracy, the user encrypts the original features using secure kNN, generating a second- round ranking index. Finally, the user sends the indexes and encrypted files to the server.

The main contributions of this paper are summarized as follows:

We propose a feasible and secure cross- modal retrieval scheme that enables fast and accurate cross- modal retrieval on encrypted data in large- scale outsourced data scenarios. This is the first attempt to combine locality- sensitive hashing with symmetric searchable encryption to address crossmodal retrieval problems. By transforming LSH values into inverted indexes, we use SSE to generate secure encrypted indexes, achieving fast retrieval while protecting privacy. We implement secure kNN to re- rank the first- round ranking result set generated by LSH, further improving the retrieval accuracy. Through analysis and experiments on real- world datasets, we demonstrate that our scheme is secure, efficient, and accurate.

The structure of the paper is as follows. In Section 2, we briefly introduce some background knowledge. In Section 3, we present the system model of the proposed scheme, define the threat model, and then explain the scheme in detail. In Section 4, we analyze the security of the scheme and provide experimental results. Finally, we conclude the paper in Section 5.

# II. PRELIMINARIES

# A. Locality-Sensitive Hashing

LSH was proposed by Gionis et al. [13] as a solution to the ANN search problem in high- dimensional data. LSH uses a family of hash functions to map similar high- dimensional vectors to the same hash bucket with high probability, while dissimilar vectors are mapped to different buckets. The scheme presented in this paper implements E2LSH [14], which generates the hash value of a vector using the following equation.

$$
\begin{array}{r}H(f) = \left[\frac{A\times f + b}{r}\right] \end{array} \tag{1}
$$

Where  $A\in R^{B\times D}$  represents a matrix satisfying the standard normal distribution,  $\pmb{b}$  is a random vector selected uniformly in  $[0,r]$  and  $r$  represents the width of the partition of  $A\times f$  projected on the real number field, that is, the size of each hash bucket in. Specifically, every input vector that passes through E2LSH is mapped to an integer vector. To facilitate subsequent processing, we constrain the range of each dimension to  $[- 2^{l - 1}$ $2^{l - 1} - 1]$  and denote the parameters of E2LSH as  $P_{\mathrm{LSH}} = \{A,b,r\}$

# B. Symmetric Searchable Encryption

SSE is capable of retrieving the encrypted inverted index of files. The SSE algorithm, denoted as SSE  $=$  (KeyGen, BuildIndex, GenTrapdoor, Search), is specifically defined as follows:

$k_{SSE}\gets \mathrm{SSE.KeyGen}(1^{\lambda})$  : It represents the key generation algorithm of SSE that takes a security parameter  $\lambda$  as input and produces a SSE secret key  $k_{SSE}$ $\tilde{I}\gets \mathrm{SSE.BuildeIndex}(I,k_{\mathrm{SSE}})$  : Given an inverted index  $I$  and the secret key  $k_{\mathrm{SSE}}$  , outputs an encrypted index  $\tilde{I}$ $\tilde{T}\gets \mathrm{SSE.GenTrapdoor}(w,k_{\mathrm{SSE}})$  : It takes a keyword  $w$  that the user wants to query and the secret key  $k_{\mathrm{SSE}}$  as input and outputs a trapdoor  $\tilde{T}$  for  $w$ $\{ID_q\} \gets \mathrm{SSE.Search}(\tilde{I},\tilde{T})$  : This function accepts an encrypted index  $\tilde{I}$  and query trapdoor  $\tilde{T}$  as input and outputs a set of document identifiers  $ID_{q}$  containing keyword  $w$

Our scheme utilizes SSE scheme of [15], which supports very large- scale data sets while having the optimal query time complexity and the minimum privacy leakage.

# C. Secure kNN

Secure kNN is a privacy- preserving computing method proposed by [14]. It enables the computation of the inner product of encrypted vectors. Secure kNN consists of the following algorithms:

$k_{\mathrm{kNN}}\gets \mathrm{Secure\_kNN.KeyGen}(1^{\lambda})$  : Secure_kNN.KeyGen is the key generation algorithm. Given the security parameter  $\lambda$  ,it outputs two random  $(d\times d)$  - dimensional invertible matrices,  $M_{1}$  and  $M_2$  ,and a  $d$  - dimensional random binary vector s.  $M_{1}$ $M_2$  and  $\mathbf{s}$  form the key  $k_{\mathrm{kNN}} = \{M_1,M_2,s\}$  of secure kNN.

$\tilde{f}\gets$  Secure_kNN.BuildIndex  $(f_{1},k_{\mathrm{kNN}})$  :Given a  $d$  dimensional vector  $\pmb{f}$  it uses  $\mathbf{s}$  to split  $f$  into two random vectors  $\{f_1,f_2\}$  .Specifically, when  $s[i] = 0$ $f_{1}[i] = f_{2}[i] = f[i]$  ,and when  $s[i] = 1,f,[i]$  and  $f_{2}[i]$  are two random numbers such that  $f_{1}[i] + f_{2}[i] = f[i]$  . Finally,  $\pmb{f}$  is encrypted as  $\tilde{f} = \{M_1^{\mathrm{T}}f_1,M_2^{\mathrm{T}}f_2\}$

$\tilde{\pmb{q}}\gets$  Secure_kNN.GenTrapdoor  $(q,k_{\mathrm{kNN}})$  :Given a  $d$  dimensional query vector  $\pmb{q}$  ,it uses  $\pmb{q}$  to split  $\pmb{q}$  into two random vectors  $\{q_{1},q_{2}\}$  . Specifically, if  $s[i] = 1$ $q_{1}[i] = q_{2}[i] = q[i]$  and if  $s[i] = 0$ $q_{1}[i]$  and  $q_{2}[i]$  are two random numbers that satisfy  $q_{1}[i] + q_{2}[i] = q[i]$  .Finally,  $\pmb{q}$  is encrypted as  $\tilde{\pmb{q}} = \{\pmb{M}_{1}^{- 1}\pmb{q}_{1},\pmb{M}_{2}^{- 1}\pmb{q}_{2}\}$

$ip\gets$  Secure_kNN.Match  $(\tilde{f},\tilde{q})$  : As shown in (2) the inner product ip of  $\tilde{f}$  and  $\tilde{q}$  is the inner product of the plaintext  $\pmb{f}$  and  $\pmb{q}$

ip = \tilde{\pmb{f}}\cdot \tilde{\pmb{q}} = \pmb {M}_1^\top \pmb {f}_1\cdot \pmb {M}_1^{-1}\pmb {q}_1 + \pmb {M}_2^\top \pmb {f}_2\cdot \pmb {M}_2^{-1}\pmb {q}_2 = \pmb {f}\cdot \pmb{q} \tag{2}

However, security can be improved to some extent by adding random noise dimensions to the index and trapdoor, at the cost of accuracy in matching. For instance, Ding et al.[18] have proposed an enhanced secure kNN method in their scheme, which can be readily integrated into our scheme.

# III. SCHEME

# A. System Model

The proposed scheme in this article includes three types of entities: Data Owner (DO), Data User (DU), and Cloud Server (CS). The system model is shown in Fig. 1.

1) DO possesses a privacy-preserving cross-modal dataset. To securely outsource the data to CS, DO will first generate the necessary keys for the scheme, then utilize CLIP to extract features from the dataset, and subsequently create a two-layer secure index locally. The encrypted index and dataset are then outsourced to CS after encryption.

2) DU acquires the keys from DO. When retrieving data, DU also needs to utilize CLIP to extract query features, then generate a trapdoor using keys, and subsequently transmit the trapdoor to CS. DU is able to decrypt and obtain relevant query-related data from the results returned by CS.

3) CS as an entity with powerful storage and computing capabilities, is responsible for storing the encrypted index and encrypted data provided by DO. Upon receiving a trapdoor from DU, CS utilizes it to retrieve the encrypted index and subsequently transmits the retrieval result to DU.

# B. Threat Model

Similar to prior research [5,7,8,9,10], we make the assumption that CS is an honest but curious entity. This means that while CS will faithfully execute protocols, it will also attempt to extract privacy information from the trapdoors and outsourced data. Furthermore, we assume that both DO and DU are fully honest entities who will not disclose keys to CS, and that the communication channel between them is secure and reliable. Based on these security assumptions, we consider the attack model to be a ciphertext- only attack (COA) model. Given this attack model, we believe that our scheme must achieve the following security goals:

File privacy: Encrypted files are outsourced to an honest but curious CS; therefore, it is essential to safeguard the privacy of plaintext data.

Trapdoor privacy: DU's query may contain private information, and the scheme should prevent attackers from deducing the original semantic information of DU's query from the uploaded trapdoors.

Index privacy: CS cannot extract any semantic content in the index plaintext from the encrypted index, including the value of the image feature or the keyword plaintext.

# C. Scheme

The proposed scheme consists of six parts: Init, EncImage, BuildIndex, GenTrapdoor, Retrieval, and Dec.

$(K,P_{\mathrm{LSH}})\leftarrow \mathrm{Init}(1^{\lambda})$  : Given the security parameter  $\lambda$  as input, Init process begins by invoking SSE.KeyGen  $m$  times to generate SSE keys  $k_{\mathrm{SSE}} = \{k_{\mathrm{SSE},j}\}_{j = 1}^{m}$  Next, Secure kNN.KeyGen is executed to generate the secure kNN key  $k_{\mathrm{kNN}} = \{M_1,M_2,s\}$  . Then, the key generation function of a traditional symmetric encryption algorithm like AES is used to produce the file encryption key sk. Additionally, LSH parameters  $P_{\mathrm{LSH}} = \{A,b,r\}$  are also generated. DO executes Init to obtain  $K = \{k_{\mathrm{SSE}},k_{\mathrm{kNN}},sk\}$  and E2LSH parameter  $P_{\mathrm{LSH}}$  and sends them through the secure channel to DU.

$\widetilde{E}\leftarrow \mathrm{EncImage}(X,sk)$  : DO's plaintext image set is represented as  $X = \{x_{i}\}_{i = 1}^{n}$  . DO encrypts the plaintext image set using AES encryption algorithm with the file encryption key  $sk$  to obtain the ciphertext image set  $\widetilde{E} = \{\widetilde{E}_i\}_{i = 1}^n$  and then sends it to CS.

$I\gets \mathrm{BuildIndex}(X,k_{\mathrm{SSE}},k_{\mathrm{kNN}},P_{\mathrm{LSH}})$  : BuildIndex is demonstrated in Algorithm 1. Initially, DO utilizes CLIP to extract features from the plaintext image set as  $\pmb {F} = \{\pmb {f}_i\}_{i = 1}^n$  , and then employs E2LSH to obtain the hash value  $\pmb {B} = \{\pmb {b}_i\}_{i = 1}^n$  of  $F$  After obtaining the hash value  $B$  for feature  $F_{\cdot}$  as illustrated in Fig. 2, the hash value  $b_{i}$  is divided into  $m$  segments  $\pmb {h} = \{h_j\}_{j = 1}^m$  with each segment  $h_j$  having a size of  $\left\lfloor \frac{H}{m}\right\rfloor$  dimensions (if the last segment is smaller than  $\left\lfloor \frac{H}{m}\right\rfloor$  , dummy dimensions can be added), and the  $\left\lfloor \frac{H}{m}\right\rfloor$  dimensions of each segment are combined as the

hash bucket value. With these specific hash bucket value, DO constructs an inverted index  $I_{j}$  for the hash values of the  $j$  - th segment of all features. Collectively, all features form inverted indexes, denoted as  $I = \{I_{j}\}_{j = 1}^{m}$  . The keyword of each inverted index  $I_{j}$  is the  $j$  - th segment hash bucket value of the feature, i.e.,  $h_j$  and the value of the index is the set of image  $IDs$  that share the same hash bucket value in the  $j$  - th segment. For example, in Fig.  $2,x_{1}$  has a hash value  $b_{1} = 0\mathrm{xFA12CD}$  and is divided into three segments, the first segment "FA" ensures that  $x_{1}$  is included in the value set corresponding to the keyword "FA" in  $I_{1}$  .After obtaining all  $m$  inverted indexes, DO can then use SSE.BuildIndex  $(h_j,k_{\mathrm{SSE},j})$  to generate an encrypted index  $\tilde{I}_j^1$  for each  $I_{j}$  corresponding to the  $j$  - th hash bucket. Next, DO performs Secure_kNN.BuildIndex on each feature  $\pmb {f}_i,$  generating an encrypted feature set  $\tilde{I} = \{\tilde{I}_i^2\}_{i = 1}^n$  for second- round ranking. Finally, the encrypted index is represented as  $\tilde{I} = \{\tilde{I}^1,\tilde{I}^2\}$  , and DO sends  $\tilde{I}$  to CS.

$\widetilde{T}_q\gets \mathrm{GenTrapdoor}(q,k_{\mathrm{SSE}},k_{\mathrm{kNN}})$  Similar to the process of DO constructing the index, DU also begins by utilizing CLIP to extract the feature vector of the query text  $q$  denoted as  $\pmb {f}_q$  during the trapdoor generation phase. The feature vector  $\pmb {f}_q$  is then divided into  $m$  blocks of keywords:  $\pmb {h}_q = \{h_{q,j}\}_{j = 1}^m$  . For each keyword  $h_{q,j}$  , SSE.GenTrapdoor is employed to generate a trapdoor  $\tilde{T}_j^1$  for retrieving the encrypted index  $\tilde{I}_j^1$  ,where  $\tilde{T}_j^1 = \mathrm{SSE.GenTrapdoor}(h_{q,j},k_{\mathrm{SSE},j})$  Next, DU applies Secure_kNN.GenTrapdoor to  $\pmb {f}_q$  which results in the generation of an encrypted query  $\tilde{T}^2 = \{\tilde{T}_i^2\}_{i = 1}^n$  for second- round ranking. Finally, DU sends the encrypted query  $\tilde{T}_q = \{\tilde{T}^1,\tilde{T}^2\}$  to CS.

$\widetilde{R}\gets \mathrm{Retrieval}(\widetilde{I},\widetilde{E},\widetilde{T}_q)$  : As shown in Algorithm 2, upon receiving the query trapdoor  $\tilde{T}_q$  from DU, CS first uses trapdoor  $\tilde{T}^1$  to retrieve the encrypted index  $\tilde{I}^1$  . # IV. EVALUATION

# A. Security Analysis

In this section, we will analyze the security of our scheme and demonstrate that it meets the security goals we have set.

File Privacy: In our scheme, each image is encrypted using AES with the file encryption key sk. Since our threat model assumes that DU and DO will not collude with CS to leak sk to CS, our scheme effectively protects the privacy of the file.

Trapdoor Privacy: Due to the modular design of our scheme, the trapdoor privacy is easily explained. In our scheme, the trapdoor generation process requires secure kNN and SSE. For secure kNN, the features input by DU are split into two random vectors  $\pmb {q}_1$  and  $\pmb {q}_2$  by vector  $\mathbf{s}$ , and then the trapdoors  $M_{1}^{- 1}q_{1}$  and  $M_2^{- 1}q_2$  are generated. In the case of a ciphertext- only attack, CS cannot decrypt the encrypted matrices  $M_{1}$  and  $M_2$ . Therefore, as long as the key  $k_{\mathrm{kNN}}$  is kept confidential, the trapdoor information will not be leaked. For SSE, the security of the trapdoors and indexes is described by the leakage function of SSE scheme. In our implementation, the trapdoors leak the search pattern, meaning CS can discern whether two queries are generated from the same input. However, we believe this leakage is acceptable, as under the threat model we have set, CS cannot derive any semantic information about the original user query from SSE trapdoors. Based on the above explanation, we believe our scheme ensures trapdoor privacy.

Index Privacy: For secure kNN, similar to trapdoor privacy, since  $k_{\mathrm{kNN}}$  is not leaked, CS cannot obtain the plaintext feature values of DO's images from the encrypted index. Therefore, secure kNN guarantees the privacy of the encrypted index during second- round ranking. As for SSE, our scheme will leak the access pattern during the query process, which refers to the encrypted images being queried and their count. However, CS finds it difficult to extract any meaningful semantic information from the encrypted index using the access pattern.

# B. Experiment settings

# C. Efficiency

1) BuildIndex: As shown in Fig. 4, we compared the performance of our scheme with PITR [12] in index construction, since MU-TEIR did not provide the time required for index construction. The index construction performance for PITR is taken from the original paper. From the graph, it can be observed that the time required for index construction increases almost linearly with the number of files. When the number of documents is the same, our scheme is approximately  $10\%$  faster than PITR. This is because our index structure is simpler and more efficient. Additionally, the independent inverted indexes allow for parallel construction of the encrypted SSE indexes, further boosting the speed.

2) Retrieval: As shown in Fig. 5, we compared the retrieval speed of our scheme with PITR. Our scheme outperforms PITR in retrieval speed, being approximately  $10\%$  faster. This demonstrates the effectiveness of the first-level index generated by our scheme. In the first round of retrieval, it filters out the majority of irrelevant images while ensuring security. We also compared our scheme with MU-TEIR [9] for image-to-image retrieval. In MU-TEIR experiments, it took 12.57 seconds to return the Top-5 results on a 1000-image dataset using 24 CPU cores—a retrieval time two orders of magnitude slower than ours, primarily due to the computational overhead of DT-PKC.

# D. Retrieval Precision

PITR utilizes a fine- tuned pre- trained cross- modal model, which outperforms non- fine- tuned counterparts in our scheme on specific datasets. However, their model can be seamlessly integrated into our scheme. Consequently, we compared the retrieval precision of our scheme with MU- TEIR on the Caltech256 dataset. We used "a photo of a label" as the query, where "label" represents the category labels in the Caltech256 dataset. We tested precision at Top- k across all labels in the dataset, calculating the average precision for each query. The precision results are shown in Fig. 6. As can be seen, our scheme outperforms MU- TEIR in terms of precision. Even for the Top- 20 returned results, we maintained a high level of precision. Moreover, our scheme is a cross- modal retrieval scheme, while MU- TEIR only supports image- to- image retrieval.

However, due to the two- layer indexing structure, our scheme employs LSH in the first- round ranking to filter out a large number of irrelevant images. In doing so, it also inadvertently filters out some relevant images, reducing the pool available for the second- round ranking. As a result, the most relevant images concentrate in the top few results (e.g., Top- 10), so increasing k may not yield significantly more relevant images and ultimately lowers precision. As illustrated in Fig. 6, the retrieval precision of our scheme decreases with increasing k at a faster rate than that of MU- TEIR. Although adding more SSE instances can mitigate this issue, balancing precision and efficiency remains a key topic for future research. We also tested our scheme's performance in cross- modal retrieval on both the Caltech256 dataset and the Cifar- 100 dataset, achieving mean average precision scores of 0.853 and 0.649, respectively. These results further demonstrate the effectiveness of our scheme.

# V. CONCLUSION

In this paper, we propose a privacy- preserving cross- modal retrieval scheme for large- scale data outsourcing scenarios. Specifically, to bridge the "semantic gap" between cross- modal data, we utilize CLIP to extract features from both images and text, mapping them into the same vector space. The scheme then applies LSH to the features to obtain hash values, enabling fast retrieval in large- scale data environments. Additionally, we introduce SSE into the scheme, reconstructing hash values to generate multiple independent inverted indexes, and using SSE to secure these indexes. To further enhance retrieval accuracy, we apply secure kNN to reorder the results from the first- round ranking. Finally, security analysis and extensive experiments conducted on widely used real- world datasets demonstrate our scheme's privacy protection, efficiency, and feasibility.
